Things Labor Unions Do for Their Members

  • Bargain with management
  • Provide pensions for retirement
  • Provide legal and other services

Key Terms Related to Labor Issues

  • Collective Bargaining: When a group comes together to negotiate terms of a contract.

  • Labor Union: An organization formed by workers of a company, or an entire industry, to counter the economic power of management.

  • Management: The leaders of a company that oversee how resources are used, including labor, to make a product.

  • Negotiation: The tactic used by one or more parties to gain their desires or demands, usually through compromise or trade-offs.

  • Mediation: When two sides cannot come to an agreement on their own, a neutral third party is called in to help move negotiations along.

  • Arbitration: When mediation does not work to resolve a conflict, both sides agree to allow the neutral third party to resolve it-either by selecting the best option provided by each side or creating a solution of their own.

  • Strike: Tactic used by workers to demonstrate their unhappiness with management by refusing to work, in an attempt to shut down operations and hurt the company financially. These can take the form of collective refusal to come into work, walking picket lines and blocking entry or showing up for work but not working. (These are called slowdown or sit-down strikes).

  • LockOut Tactic used by management to prevent slow-down or sit down strikes by workers. This forces any labor action to occur off the company's premisis.

  • Picket Line: Tactic used by labor to express their frustration with management by attempting to bar entry into the company by both employees and customers.

  • Strike Breakers: Tactic used by management to hire replacement workers while a strike is underway. They are usually unemployed workers and offered higher wages during the strike to undermine a union's collective will. They often are forced to cross picket lines.

  • Injunction: Legal tactic used by either side in a labor dispute, but usually by management. An argument is filed with a local court asking for a judge to end a strike or lockout. While negotiations continue often calls for mediation or arbitration to resolve conflict.

  • Closed Shop: A business, or an entire industry, that requires employees to belong to the union that represents their job. Usually a condition of employment before being hired.

  • Open Shop A buisness, or an entire industry, that does not require employees to belong to the union tht represents their job. Employees have the option to joing the union, or not.

  • Sherman Antitrust Act (1890): Purpose of this law: Made it illegal for every combination of companies or conspiracy among firms that restrains (or restricts) trade or commerce. Trusts and monopolies were made "Illegal". Natural and government monopolies were explained.

  • Clayton Antitrust Act (1914): Purpose of this law: Established the following as rights for workers: Right to strike, Right to picket, Right to organize. Unions were made exempt from antitrust laws.

  • Wagner Act (National Labor Relations Act (1935): Purpose of this law: Reaffirmed the right of workers to form unions, Established the right to collective bargaining (in the private sector only), Established the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB), that provides arbitrators to resolve conflicts between management and labor.

  • Fair Labor Standards Act (1938): Established the first national minimum wage ($0.25 per hour in 1938, or $3.80 per in today's dollars). Established the maximum number of hours worked in a week (40)

  • Taft- hartley Act (1947): Prohibied closed shops in the workplace, Allowed states to pass right-to-work laws, made injuctions against strikes by unions easier to get for management.

  • Wisconsin Statute 111.70 (1959): Public employees right to collectively bargain is recongnized by the state of wisconsin. Unions represent employees to negtiate for highter wages and safer working conditions. Additinally public entities (ex. cities, towns and villages , ect...) are required to bargain with public employees and their unions.

  • Landrum-Griffin Act (1959L): This requires union leaders to be elected via a democratic election. Established oversight by the federal goverment and audit of union pension funds by the U.S. labor deparment.

  • Civil Rights Act of 1964: First federal program that banned discrimination based on race, sex, color, or religion. Established the equal employment oppourtunities commision (eeoc) to enforce the provisions of this act.

  • Wisconsin Act 10 (2011): As a solution to the effects of the great recession, collective bargaining right are stripped from public employees, except for pay (which is limited to the previous years inflation rate). Members of police and firefighters unions are exempted from this act.

  • Wisconsin Act 1 (2015): Wisconsin becomes the 25th state to pass right-to-work legislation for private sector jobs. Private sector union members are no longer forced to pay "Fair Share" dues. Collective bargaining rights are comprimised for private sector employees.
